The Monte Carlo Method

Monte Carlo simulations use random selection to aproach an accurate solution. Since the sampling is randomised, the accuracy of a Monte Carlo simulation increases with the number of samples at the cost of an increased computational load.

Strictly speaking, pseudo-random selection is normally used in the Monte Carlo method, since a traditional computer is incapable of generating truly random numbers.

Demonstration: Estimating Pi

The value of ${\pi}$ can be estimated using the Monte Carlo method:

  1. Consider a circle with a radius of ${r}$, bounded by a square. The ratio of the area of the circle and the area of the square must therefore be: ${\frac{\pi r^{2}}{4r^{2}} = \frac{\pi}{4}}$.
  2. Symmetry reduce the problem to a quadrant, enclosed by a smaller square of width r. The ${\frac{\pi}{4}}$ ratio still holds in this configuration.
  3. Consider that the circle and square are centred about an origin (0,0) and that ${r=1}.
  4. Sample randomly within this space, recording the coordinates.
  5. Via the Pythagorean theorem (${a^{2} + b^{2} = c^{2}}$). Samples that have a disance from the origin of less than, or equal to, 1 must be within the quadrant. Categorise each sample accordingly.
  6. Divide the number of samples within the quadrant by the total number of samples. This is performing the same circle/square division calculation as above, albeit using pointwise sampling of the area.
  7. Multiply the result by 4, to estimate ${\pi}$.
